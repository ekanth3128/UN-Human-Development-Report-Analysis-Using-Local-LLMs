Moreover, the high fertility future would not make much difference
compared to the baseline scenario in districts with higher share of old-
er population and/or disturbed sex structure in the prime reproductive
ages due to steady out-migration. Figure 7 simply summarises these
two types of districts, presented by two extreme cases – Belgrade and
Bor. On the other hand, zero migration scenario would have much
stronger effect on both types of districts, but of opposite direction.
When Fig. 6 and Fig. 7 are compared, a clear conclusion emerges: The
effect of migration is much more relevant for the subnational spatial units
than for the national level. In other words, policies stimulating higher birth
numbers will eventually, though after quite a long period, have effects at
the national level, but at the expense of most districts which are affected by
out-migration and rapidly ageing population. This also means that birth
stimulating policies will have almost no effect in most areas of the country
unless coupled with policies aimed at more balanced spatial development
that would reduce the prominent gaps in net migration between districts.
When it comes to population ageing, the forecast ageing ratios report
a mixed bag of news. High fertility future can make some benefits in
comparison to the baseline scenario, but they could be visible only
by 2100 and only in small number of districts due to the very na-
ture of the fertility rise impact on the age structure. It takes at least
20 years for the first projected new-borns to enter the working-age
group and start reducing the economic pressure. Until then, they
themselves also contribute to the ‘burden’ on economically active
population. Moreover, as total fertility rates are forecast to quickly
grow by the-mid-century and remain high until the end of the projec-
