The subsequent sharp fall in the period TFR in Serbia began in the
late 1980s (Nikitović, 2016b). Although a steeper fall could have been
expected due to the wars and the institutional crisis during the 1990s,
the total fertility rate in Serbia did not experience the lowest levels as
was the case with most former socialist states in Europe after 1990.
Moreover, the war setting could be a reason that contributed to pro-
longed consistency of traditional values associated with family and
childbearing (Sardon, 2001; Rašević, 2004; Petrović, 2011).
The total fertility rate in Serbia has been oscillating between 1.4 and
1.5 since 2005. On the other hand, most states that belonged to the
lowest-low fertility group have recently experienced a rise in TFR
(e.g. Slovenia, Latvia, and Hungary have exceeded 1.5, and Czechia
even reached 1.7), which could be explained by the expected slow-
down in the pace of the ‘postponement transition’, i.e. the transition
from early to late childbearing ages as suggested by Goldstein et al.
(2009). Although this transition in Serbia started almost at the same
time as in the lowest-low-fertility countries, its current pace is lagging
behind them, according to the mean age at first birth, thus suggesting
the period TFR might start increasing once the postponement slows.
Nevertheless, the completed cohort fertility rate is likely to decline
as a result of later childbearing (Kohler et al., 2002). The 2011 cen-
sus indicated that a long period in which this indicator was stable in
Serbia came to an end, as the average number of live births given by
the cohorts of women who were approaching the end of their repro-
ductive age in 2011 substantially dropped – from 1.80 to 1.55 (Rašević
& Galjak, 2022).
Since the late 1980s, the fertility rates of women younger than 25 be-
gan to decline sharply in Serbia, while those of women older than 30
started to increase, though at a slower pace (Nikitović et al., 2019).
Consequently, the average age of childbearing increased from 25.9
to 30.1 years, and the same indicator at first birth from 23.9 to 28.8
between 1991 and 2020. Also, the 30-34 age group has currently the
highest fertility rate for the first time in the last 60 years, though on
account of only two largest districts whose centres are the biggest cit-
ies in the country (Belgrade and Novi Sad).
The postponement of the first birth has been a significant factor in
the low fertility rate, which is nowadays a concern for many European
countries (Schmidt et al., 2012). The change in cumulative fertility by
age in Serbia was considerably more affected by the increasing share
of women who did not give birth than by the reproductive pattern of
those who did (Penev & Stanković, 2021). The increase in childless-
ness is mostly caused by the delay in the birth of the first child, but
also by the increase in the permanent childlessness of women in the
late pro-creative years (Rašević, 2015). The general childlessness rate15
was relatively stable in Serbia until 1991 (30.1%), when it began to in-
crease intensely, reaching 41.6% in 2011 and 43.4% in 2020. The al-
ready high share of childless women aged 30–34 according to the 2011
census (30.6%) increased by 20% until 2020 (36.5%). This indicator
rose by as much as 30% in the group aged 45-49, indicating currently
the 13.8% of permanently childless women (Penev & Stanković, 2021).
