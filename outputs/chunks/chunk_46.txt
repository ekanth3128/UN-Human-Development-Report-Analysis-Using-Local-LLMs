Aside from possible economic impacts of population decline, there
are also widely discussed geopolitical implications from the shifts
in relative population sizes due to rapid growth in some parts of the
world – in particular in Africa – combined with declines in other parts
(such as countries in Europe). But as we have discussed in the intro-
duction and illustrated in several other sections, it is the headcount
that matters, but rather what is inside the heads that matters most, i.e.
the human capital mostly in the form of education and skills. Figure 8

of educational attainment, with reconstructed data back to 1950 and
projections according to the “medium assumption” scenarios from
the most recent population scenarios produced by joint efforts of the

Figure 8 contrasts the two European regions (East and West, using
the UN regional definitions) with relatively stable population sizes
against the rapidly growing continents of Asia and Africa. While the
population of Eastern Europe (including Russia) is clearly on a declin-
ing trajectory in terms of total population (from over 300M around
2000 down to less than 250M projected by 2075), the population
with post-secondary education is quite strongly expanding to almost
100M. Given the overriding importance of human capital discussed
above, this is actually a quite promising future trend and looks cer-
tainly much less negative than the total population numbers alone
would imply through a focus on shrinkage.
In Western Europe the trend is not so different, with the difference of
some further projected population growth, mostly due to the assump-
tion of continued migration inflows. The projected expansion of the
population with post-secondary education is more rapid in Western
Europe than in Eastern Europe due to stronger recent investments in
higher education, if trends continue. But there is no reason why East-
ern Europe could not try to also pick up speed in its future education
expansion, and already the ratio of non-workers to workers in Eastern
European countries is often at a more favourable balance compared
to Western Europe.
