The rising negative impact of natural change on the total population
size of Serbia since 1992 had been only partially mitigated by the mod-
est positive migration balance by 2000. However, this net immigra-
tion during the 1990s wars in the region of the former Yugoslavia had a
controversial effect on the total population of Serbia. It was a peculiar
result of a strong inflow of refugees from the region of Yugoslavia on
the one side and somewhat lower migration outflow from the country
on the orher side. As these two flows had substantially differed in age
patterns due to the different nature of their migration drivers, the re-
sulting net migration age structure helped increase the the median age
of total population in Serbia (Penev, 2006; Nikitović & Lukić, 2010).
Moreover, net emigration joined the negative natural change in reduc-
ing the total population size of the country at the beginning of the 21st
century. The migration contributed to this drop by at least 15% or at most
26% between the census years of 2002 and 2011 (Nikitović et. al, 2015:
101). The trend of net emigration certainly continued after 2011 accord-
ing to the recent estimates (UNFPA Serbia, 2019; Nikitović, 2019).

1.1.2	 Regional differences in population
distribution
The average population density in Serbia (about 93 inh./km2) accord-
ing to the 2011 census is almost double the world average (about 50
inh./km2), but typical for the countries of South and East Europe. It
is similar to those in the neighbouring countries – somewhat higher
than in Croatia, Bulgaria and Romania, and a bit lower than in Hun-
gary or Slovenia. However, the population distribution is markedly
uneven across the country. The population density in the regions of
Vojvodina (89), Šumadija and West Serbia (77), and South and East
Serbia (60) is below the national average, while in the region of the
capital city (Belgrade) it is several times higher, with an average of
514 inh./km2 and a peak of 18.8 thousand inhabitants per km2 in the
urban core of the city. At the same time, around 43% of the total pop-
ulation of Serbia has lived in the high-density zone covering 20% of
the country (Figure 3a).
