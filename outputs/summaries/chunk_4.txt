
This section reviews Serbia's demographic challenges, with a focus on the country's shrinking workforce amidst increasing emigration. Over recent decades, millions have left; as many as two million in total since Yugoslavia dissolved (2). From peak migration to Italy and Austria between 1961-71 of around three percent per year due to economic shifts from agriculture to industry/services sector demands for a different skill set (3), emigration rates have fluctuated but never reached pre-World War II levels.

The current migration flow is less about labor demand and more about personal reasons, with over 60% of the population aged between twenty and thirty being mobile in search of better life conditions or due to family reunification (4). Despite this trend's influence on Serbia’s demographics, policies focusing solely on economic factors are insufficient. To tackle depopulation effectively from a familial standpoint requires addressing the cultural and institutional barriers that discourage work-life balance within family life (5).

The report highlights several policy recommendations: Improving living conditions in rural areas to retain youth, strengthening educational systems for better skill matching with employment sectors like IT or tourism where job opportunities are abundant. Furthermore, it stresses the need for government support and flexible family-friendly work policies (6). By doing so, Serbia can ensure that families feel secure enough not to seek overseas migration as an escape route but rather a place of stable growth opportunity within their own country (7)(8).

In conclusion, strategic investments in education and infrastructure for child development alongside promoting gender-neutral family roles could serve as potential solutions. By creating an environment conducive to raising families at home while also working productively, Serbia can tackle the depopulation issue from within its own borders (9)(10).
