
Population ageing is altering demographic trends in Serbia, with significant implications for healthcare and social security systems. In the latest UN HDR report, it's highlighted that mortality rates are shifting towards older populations; life expectancy at birth stands at a respectable average of 76 years but varies across genders—81 for women to 72 for men (UNDP data). The main causes of death include cardiovasc0.9D\xADrus disease and cancer, with non-communicable diseases rising as primary culprits in Serbia's demographic profile.

The report underscores the burden on healthcare amidst COVID-19 but also notes an increase in telemedicine usage for maintaining care continuity (SAP Reports). For pensions, it suggests transition to a notional defined contribution system and privatization of second pillar schemes as responses. Concerns over old-age poverty have led policy recommendations such as the GMIOP scheme that offers financial assistance to seniors beyond basic social services—acknowledging gaps in current FSA provisions (UN ESCWA).

The report also touches on depopulation, indicating Serbia's shrinking cities and rural exodus could intensify. Urban planning efforts are recommended for revitalizing these areas to curb economic decline linked with demographic shifts—a key spatial element in the nation’s development (European Policies).

In summary, the HDR report emphasizes Serbia's need to adapt its social security and healthcare policies amidst an ageing population while addressing depopulation challenges through strategic urban planning.
