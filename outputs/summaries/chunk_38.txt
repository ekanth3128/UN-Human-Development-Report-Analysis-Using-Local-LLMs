In Eastern European contexts, notably in nations such as Serbia within Central and Eastern Europe (CEE), there's a significant brain drain with annual losses amounting up to around 1.2 billion Euros—this is especially evident among the youth who are gravitating towards Western economies for better opportunities due to an entrenched belief that local prospects equate to failure, as reported by social psychologists and media (Government of Serbia, 2018; Simić, 2018). This departure is driven both by economic needs—in search of higher pay and job opportunities abroad—and personal connections. Returnees often express a revaluation of home's quality-of-life aspects over material wealth once they experience Western lifestyles (Brezjanović-Shogren, 2019). While youth emigration is partly due to an uninspired cultural narrative suggesting local staying equals losing out socially and economically, the resultant labor deficits are pronounced in crucial sectors such as healthcare. Despite producing a surplus of medical professionals relative to OECD standards (Institute for Public Health of Serbia, 2015), there’s an emerging shortage due to emigration and potential career changes within the sector itself; with higher-skilled professions like IT specialists also showing significant trends towards outmigration. From a macroeconomic perspective, regional studies indicate that over two decades of continued migration resulted in approximately 7% GDP losses for CEE countries (Atoyan et al., 2016). These emigrations underscore the importance of aligning Serbia's education system more closely with market demands and local needs.
