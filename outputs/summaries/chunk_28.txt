
In UN Human Development Reports of late, demographers have been reassessing their assumptions about future global fertility levels due to evolving data since the influential report's projections in the '60s and early '70s. These original estimates presumed a rectangular survival curve with static TFR at 2.1, leading many into believing populations would stabilize globally without significant increases or decreases (Myrskylä et al., 2009). However, recent trends demonstrate that life expectancy continues to rise beyond initial estimates and fertility rates have declined further than expected in nearly all countries. Notably, South Korea's TFR plummeted from a high of approximately 5 children per woman around the mid-20th century to its current level below replacement rate at just over 1 child since it reached modernization (Vollset et al., 2neralize this pattern across Europe and Northern America, where even countries with developed economies have experienced significant declines. These shifts suggest that fertility levels will not stabilize as once predicted; rather, they are likely to continue their downward trend unless new policies emerge (United Nations Population Division 2019).
