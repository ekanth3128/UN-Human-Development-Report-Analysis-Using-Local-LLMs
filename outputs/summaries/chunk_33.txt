The UN Human Development Report highlights Serbia's educational advancements over recent decades. In 1990, average schooling was about 9.91 years among working-age individuals; by 2015 it increased to almost 12 years (Reiter et al., 2020). Actual skill test results also show improvement from 9.10 years in 1990 to 12.14 years in the same year, slightly surpassing OECD averages by a small margin today. These gains demonstrate Serbia's progress towards enhancing its human capital amidst ageing population dynamics that pose macroeconomic challenges for national systems of contributions and pensions—areas where demographic dependency ratios play an essential role in economic assessments, yet they are based on outdated assumptions such as the static contribution end point at 65. The report suggests refining these measures to reflect current realities like increased elderly productivity through healthcare delivery roles and redefines age groups for a more accurate portrayal of dependency ratios in Serbia's demographic landscape (Mirdal, Bélanger, & Lutz, 2020).
