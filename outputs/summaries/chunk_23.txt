In ancient Greece, Plato speculated about an optimal city-state population of 5,040 households. This ideal number was chosen because it is divisible by many numbers including 12, which held sacred significance in Greek culture (Goswami, 1985). During the Middle Ages and into early modern times, concerns about overpopulation were common; for instance, Henry IV of England tied a nation's wealth to its population size around AD 1400. Similar ideas influenced thinkers such as Ibn Khaldun (Tunis), Graunt (England), Petty and Halley (England), and Süssmilch (Prussia) in the 17th century, who advocated for measures like taxing celibacy or regulating out-migration. In his seminal work "An Essay on the Principle of Population" published around AD 1798, Robert Malthus posited that human populations grow exponentially while food supply increases linearly due to fixed land and diminishing returns from labor (Malthus, 1798). He feared this imbalance would lead to famine-induced population checks. However, modern demography highlights the critical role of reducing child mortality for increasing populations in contrast with historical perspectives limited by medical knowledge at that time.

Policies advocating taxes on celibacy and rewards for marriage/parenthood were discussed historically as means to manage population growth (Goswami, 1985; Stangeland, 1904). Malthus's theory suggests a direct link between excessive reproduction and resource scarcity. Early modern European thinkers like Graunt compiled the "Bills of Mortality," revealing high child mortality rates in London (Graunt, 1662), emphasizing that reducing child death is key to population growth rather than discouraging births or celibacy as feared by Malthus.

UN Human Development Reports often call for policies enhancing education and healthcare due to their positive impact on fertility decline, without encouraging early marriage—a marked contrast from historical views (UNDP 2019). These reports advocate strategies like improving maternal care which can lead to lower infant mortality rates rather than penalizing celibacy or promoting incentives for larger families.
