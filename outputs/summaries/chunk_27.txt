
Since 1980, Finland's birth rates have decreased from about 25 to a current rate of less than two children per woman (TFR < 1.3), while mortality has continued its downward trend in the country with an average annual decline over time ranging between 0.6% and 1%. The demographic transition theory predicts fertility will continue falling until it stabilizes around or below replacement level, which is approximately 2.1 children per woman (Svedberg & Lutz, 2019). Despite this prediction, Finland's birth rates have remained consistently low since the mid-20th century without signs of a fertility rebound, even though its mortality rate remains among one of the world’s lowest. Scholars identify shifting societal values and changes in union patterns as possible explanations for this persistent sub-replacement level birth rates (Lesthaeghe & Van de Kaa, 2010). Mortality decline began earlier than fertility did – around 1870 according to the UN data. It took approximately 50 years afterwards before Finland's TFR started its steep descent beginning in the early 20th century (Svedberg & Lutz, 2019). Mortality decline and fertility drop are linked via social changes as people invest more resources into fewer children over time. The report emphasizes that while demographic transition is a robust theory with predictive power for mortality rates post-transition, its applicability to future birth rate trends remains unproven (Svedberg & Lutz, 2019).

Acknowledging the complexity of fertility decline in contemporary societies and possible irreversibility as suggested by demographic transition theory is crucial for understanding population dynamics. This report advises governments to adapt policies that reflect these evolving trends (Svedberg & Lutz, 2019). The Republic of Moldova exemplifies a non-revertible fertility decline post its Soviet Union dissolution where birth rates fell below even the lowest estimates from demographic transition theory. Nonetheless, despite recent slight recoveries and potential social changes driving another round of subdued childbearing decisions in Europe (Lesthaeghe & Van de Kaa, 2010), fertility trends remain low since early transitional countries' levels by far (Svedberg & Lutz, 2019). The demographic transition theory suggests that while mortality decline is irreversible in the long-term perspective of human populations, it does not guarantee an unchanging pattern for fertility rates.
