
In Serbia's Human Development Report, children in daycares make up just under a quarter of all out-of-school youth aged between six and fourteen. Despite an increase in attendance over recent years (from 20% to around 35%), this figure is still below the European average by nearly half. The limited availability contributes significantly to social integration challenges, particularly for young unemployed parents, mainly mothers, who are disadvantaged due to lack of access and financial constraints (Pavlović Babić, 2022). Family-centric policies like tax reliefs or adapted transportation systems remain inadequate. Serbia's educational system faces chronic weaknesses as indicated by PISA scores; students lag half a year behind OECD and EU peers in reading, mathematics, and science (Pavlović Babić, 2022). Though progress has been made since the early '90s, disparities persist especially among socio-economically disadvantaged groups like Roma children. As such, educational inclusivity must be enhanced to tackle these demographic challenges (Teodorović et al., 2015; Vuković, 2XX).
