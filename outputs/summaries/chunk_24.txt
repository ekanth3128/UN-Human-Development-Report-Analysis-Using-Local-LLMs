In Serbia's Human Development Report for 2022, demographic change is a central theme explored through historical to contemporary lenses (UN HDR report). Since Malthus’ era in the early 1970s and Ehrlich's subsequent publications like "The Population Bomb" &amp; "The Population Explosion", global concerns about rapid population growth have been prevalent. The French demographic decline post-1871, linked to their low fertility rates in preceding decades which led Dumont (1906) and others cite as influential factors driving these fears; a concept known today as "social capillarity". This pattern is observed globally, especially within the European Union. In Serbia's context, this trend translates into low fertility rates below 2 children per woman (UN HDR report). As of recent data in 2017 and up to December 31, 2decade-old demographic projections forecast a further decline by nearly one million people over the next decade alone. The Report highlights that these changes are largely due not to nature but personal choices influenced heavily by modern life's effects on reproduction behavior including fertility preferences and migration patterns, with urbanization playing an increasing role in such dynamics (UN HDR report). As a result of this demographic shift towards smaller families, the country faces challenges related to aging populations, shrinking workforces, decreased tax base affecting pensions &amp; social security systems. The Report suggests policies aimed at improving family support and financial incentives for having more children as potential solutions (UN HDR report).
